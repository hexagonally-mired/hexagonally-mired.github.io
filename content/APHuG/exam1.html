Q1
Unit 1 Skill 1A ★ WEAK
Stimulus
A thematic map of Sub-Saharan Africa uses color gradients to show countries by total population, with darker shades indicating larger populations. Nigeria appears the darkest shade, while several island nations appear nearly white.

Which type of map is being described, and what is a key limitation of using this type of map for geographic analysis?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ C — Choropleth Map

The description — color gradients applied to country-level areal units — defines a choropleth map. The classic limitation is the Modifiable Areal Unit Problem (MAUP): a large country like Niger looks dominant even if its population density is low, while a tiny, dense country may appear insignificant. This distortion is especially misleading when raw totals rather than rates or densities are mapped.

Skill 1A: geographic representations — know the five major thematic map types (choropleth, dot density, proportional symbol, isoline, cartogram) and the strength/limitation of each.
Q2
Unit 1 Skill 1D ★ WEAK

A geographer studying deforestation in the Amazon basin uses satellite imagery at 1:5,000,000 scale. A local environmental NGO uses aerial photographs at 1:25,000 scale to map individual clearings. Which statement correctly compares the geographic utility of these two scales?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ B — Scale Comparison

Map scale is counterintuitive: a larger denominator (1:5,000,000) = smaller scale = more area shown with less detail. A smaller denominator (1:25,000) = larger scale = less area shown with greater detail. The satellite imagery is ideal for spotting broad deforestation trends across the basin; the aerial photos reveal individual clearing boundaries. This is a direct application of geographic scale.

Skill 1D: scale of analysis — large-scale vs. small-scale maps are a frequent exam trap. Memorize: bigger denominator = smaller scale = less detail = more area.

Unit 1 Skill 1A ★ WEAK

A cartographer creates a world map using the Mercator projection for a maritime navigation chart. Which of the following best explains a limitation of using the Mercator projection for teaching global population distribution?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ E — Mercator Distortion

The Mercator projection preserves shape (conformal) but severely distorts area at high latitudes. Greenland appears comparable in size to Africa, yet Africa is about 14× larger. For navigation (constant compass bearings = straight lines), Mercator is ideal. For population distribution or political significance, it creates a perceptual bias favoring high-latitude, less-populated regions. This is a core Skill 1A concept: every projection involves a trade-off.

Q8
Unit 4 Skill 2C

Which of the following best explains why centrifugal forces present a greater challenge to state sovereignty in multinational states than in nation-states?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ D — Multinational States & Centrifugal Forces

Centrifugal forces pull states apart — ethnic tension, separatism, linguistic difference. In a nation-state (one nation, one state — e.g., Japan, Iceland), cultural homogeneity provides cohesion. In a multinational state (multiple nations within one state — e.g., India, Russia, Nigeria), competing national identities create persistent centrifugal pressure. The Kurds in Turkey/Iraq/Syria, Catalonia in Spain, and Quebec in Canada are classic examples.

Unit 1 Skill 1D ★ WEAK

A geographer analyzes crime patterns in Chicago at the neighborhood level, finding high concentrations in specific blocks. When the same data is aggregated to the city level, the pattern disappears into an average. This phenomenon is best described as which of the following?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ C — MAUP

The Modifiable Areal Unit Problem (MAUP) describes how the results of spatial analysis change depending on the areal units used (scale) and how those units are drawn (zonation). Aggregating fine-grained block data to city-wide data masks the neighborhood cluster — an important limitation of all geographic analysis. This is a Skill 1D (scale of analysis) concept directly tested on the AP exam.

Skill 1D: MAUP is the #1 concept connecting scale of analysis to data distortion. It appears on both MCQ and FRQ.
Q11
Unit 2

According to Ravenstein's Laws of Migration, which of the following patterns would be most expected?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ E — Ravenstein's Laws

Ravenstein's key laws: (1) most migrants move short distances; (2) migration proceeds step-by-step (village → small town → large city); (3) every migration stream generates a counterstream; (4) urban residents migrate less than rural residents; (5) women dominate short-distance internal migration; (6) economic motivators dominate. Choice E captures laws 1, 2, and the counterstream principle — the most comprehensive correct answer.

✓ D — Aging Population Consequences

A narrow-based pyramid signals low fertility and aging. Japan is the global archetype: its TFR (~1.2) is well below replacement (2.1), producing a top-heavy pyramid. Q22
Unit 6

The Burgess Concentric Zone Model predicts that low-income housing is located adjacent to the Central Business District (CBD), while higher-income housing is located on the urban periphery. Which of the following correctly explains why this pattern develops?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ C — Burgess Model Explanation

The Burgess model emerged from early 20th-century Chicago, where automobile ownership was not universal. Lower-income workers who walked or used streetcars to reach factory jobs had to live nearby, in Zone 2 (zone of transition) — older housing with environmental disamenities. As incomes rose, residents "filtered out" to newer housing farther from the CBD. This explains the model's structure, though it applies best to pre-WWII U.S. cities and is critiqued for not fitting Latin American, European, or contemporary auto-dependent cities.

In which way has the development of genetically modified organisms (GMOs) most directly challenged traditional subsistence farming practices in developing nations?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ E — GMO Seeds & Farmer Dependency

Traditional seed-saving — where farmers keep a portion of their harvest to replant — is incompatible with patented GMO "terminator" seeds, which are either sterile or legally restricted from replanting. Lingua franca languages like English, Swahili, and Mandarin serve an important geographic function. Which of the following best describes this function?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ B — Lingua Franca Function

A lingua franca is any language adopted as a common means of communication between speakers whose native languages differ. Unit 1Skill 2C ★

Tobler's First Law of Geography states that "everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than distant things." Which geographic concept does this law most directly support?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ C — Spatial Interaction & Distance Decay

Tobler's First Law is the foundational statement of spatial autocorrelation and distance decay — the principle that interaction, similarity, and influence between places diminishes as distance increases. This underlies gravity models, migration patterns, diffusion studies, and trade flows. Distance decay explains why Chicago influences suburbs more than cities 1,000 miles away, why diseases diffuse outward from hearths, and why cultural traits cluster spatially.

The Human Development Index (HDI) is considered a more comprehensive measure of development than GDP per capita alone because it incorporates which additional dimensions?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ D — HDI Components

HDI = three dimensions: (1) long and healthy life → life expectancy at birth; (2) knowledge → mean years of schooling + expected years of schooling; (3) decent standard of living → GNI per capita (PPP). Which of the following best explains the demographic impact of the epidemiological transition on mortality patterns in Stage 2 countries?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ E — Epidemiological Transition Stage 2

The epidemiological transition (Omran) explains shifts in disease burden across DTM stages. Q30
Unit 3

The diffusion of Buddhism from its hearth in northern India to China, Japan, and Korea is best described as which type of diffusion?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ B — Relocation Diffusion

Buddhism spread primarily through relocation diffusion — missionaries, monks, and traders physically moved along the Silk Road and maritime trade routes, carrying the faith to new regions (China via Central Asia, Korea from China, Japan from Korea). This distinguishes it from contagious diffusion (which spreads through direct contact in all directions, like a disease) or hierarchical diffusion (which spreads through an urban/power hierarchy first). The Silk Road pattern is geographically linear, not radial.

Which of the following best describes the process of suburbanization and its primary causes in post-World War II United States?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ D — Post-WWII Suburbanization Causes

Post-WWII suburbanization was multi-causal: (1) FHA/VA loan guarantees made mortgages accessible to veterans; (2) the 1956 Federal Aid Highway Act funded the Interstate system, enabling car-based commuting; (3) mass automobile production made cars affordable; (4) "white flight" — racial prejudice and blockbusting drove white families from integrating urban neighborhoods. The "resource curse" or "paradox of plenty" describes a phenomenon where countries rich in natural resources often experience worse economic development outcomes than resource-poor nations. Which of the following best explains this paradox?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ C — Resource Curse

Q40
Unit 2

Thomas Malthus argued that population growth would inevitably outpace food supply. Which of the following developments most directly contradicts the Malthusian prediction?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ B — Technology vs. Malthus

Malthus (1798) predicted geometric population growth would outstrip arithmetic food production growth, leading to famine, disease, and war as "positive checks." What he didn't foresee: agricultural technology. The Haber-Bosch process (synthetic nitrogen), HYV seeds, mechanization, and irrigation enabled food production to grow exponentially, not arithmetically. Global food output per capita has actually risen since 1800 despite enormous population growth. Neo-Malthusians argue we're approaching new limits in water, soil, and climate.

Intensive subsistence agriculture differs from extensive commercial agriculture primarily in which of the following ways?

✓ C — Intensive vs. Extensive Agriculture

The key variables: land area per farm, labor intensity, and output destination. Intensive subsistence (paddy rice in Asia) = small land, high labor, feeds family. ✓ E — Colonial Language Diffusion

Q43
Unit 6Big Idea: Impact

Which of the following best explains the environmental impact of urban heat islands in large cities?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ A — Urban Heat Island

The urban heat island (UHI) effect is caused by three interrelated processes: (1) impervious surfaces store more heat than soil/vegetation; (2) loss of tree cover eliminates the evapotranspiration cooling effect; (3) anthropogenic heat from cars, HVAC systems, and industry. Cities like Phoenix, Los Angeles, and New York have documented heat islands. Solutions include green roofs, urban tree canopy, and cool pavement. The UHI has disproportionate public health impacts on low-income residents who lack air conditioning.

Q44
Unit 4

Which of the following best illustrates the concept of electoral geography through gerrymandering?

Correct Answer & Explanation
✓ D — Gerrymandering

Gerrymandering is the manipulation of district boundaries to favor one political party. The two main tactics: "packing" (concentrating the other party's voters into as few districts as possible so they win overwhelmingly but waste votes) and "cracking" (splitting the other party's concentration across multiple districts where they can never win a majority). The result: a party can win far more seats than its statewide vote share would proportionally suggest. It's named after Massachusetts Governor Elbridge Gerry (1812).

✓ Format: [Policy] → [mechanism for reducing informal settlements] + [specific limitation of that policy]. Both halves must be explained, not just named, for the full point. This tests nuanced Impact big idea reasoning — understanding that policies have unintended consequences.

Impact tip: When asked about a policy's impact, always follow this structure: "Policy X reduces [problem] by [mechanism]. However, a limitation is [consequence] because [explanation]." This structure guarantees you address both sides.

Scoring Guide — Part G (1 point)

To earn the point: Define urban primacy + apply to Lagos using data from Stimulus 2.

Urban primacy describes a settlement pattern in which one dominant city contains a disproportionate share of a country's population, economic activity, and political infrastructure — typically at least twice the size of the second-largest city. Lagos illustrates this for Nigeria: with 15.3 million in its metro area, it far surpasses Kano (Nigeria's second city at ~3.5 million), concentrating banking, manufacturing, and media functions in a single primate city. This concentration channels investment and human capital away from Nigeria's other regions, widening spatial inequality between Lagos and the country's interior — a pattern common in countries that experienced colonial urbanization centered on coastal export ports.

✓ Must: (1) define primacy; (2) apply to Lagos using data; (3) note the geographic consequence of primacy. Stating only that Lagos is large does not earn the point — the explanatory concept of primacy and its consequences must be present.
