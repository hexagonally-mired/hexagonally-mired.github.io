Q1
Unit 1Skill 1A

An isoline map of annual precipitation across South Asia uses lines connecting points of equal rainfall. A geographer wants to add a second variable — population density — to the same map. Which of the following best explains why overlaying a choropleth layer on an isoline map can cause misinterpretation?

Correct Answer
✓ B

Choropleth boundaries follow political units (states, districts) while isolines follow continuous natural phenomena (rainfall contours). Overlaying them visually suggests that, say, a state boundary coincides with a rainfall threshold — which is geographically meaningless. This is a nuanced Skill 1A issue: map layering can create false apparent correlations between datasets that have no causal relationship.

1A: Map overlay misinterpretation is a step up from simply identifying map types — you need to understand how combining layers can distort spatial inference.

Unit 1Skill 2C

A geographer observes that the spatial diffusion of social media use follows a hierarchical pattern, spreading first to global cities (New York, London, Tokyo), then to regional centers, before reaching smaller towns. Which process best explains why internet adoption consistently follows this urban hierarchy rather than spreading equally in all directions?

Correct Answer
✓ C

Hierarchical diffusion moves through a ranked system — from most important to least — rather than radiating equally outward. The urban hierarchy (global → national → regional → local) channels information, capital, and technology downward. Social media, mobile banking, and fast food franchises all follow this pattern. The explanation (infrastructure + capital + density) is what makes C the complete geographic answer rather than simply naming the diffusion type.

Q12
Unit 4

The Heartland Theory (Mackinder, 1904) argued that control of the Eurasian "pivot area" would confer world dominance. Which of the following critiques of Heartland Theory is most geographically valid in the contemporary world?

Correct Answer
✓ B

The most valid contemporary critique of Heartland Theory is that geopolitical power is no longer primarily determined by territorial land control. Nuclear weapons make direct invasion of major powers suicidal. Air power (Spykman's Rimland counter-thesis) showed sea and air superiority matter more than interior land. Today, control of undersea internet cables, satellite networks, and global financial systems rivals territory in strategic value. The theory wasn't useless — it predicted Cold War competition — but its land-centric assumptions are increasingly anachronistic.

Correct Answer

Bid-rent theory explains land use patterns by predicting that different users will pay different maximum rents depending on their distance from the city center. Which of the following correctly applies bid-rent theory to explain why flower cultivation for export (cut flowers) in the Kenyan highlands ships to European markets despite distance, rather than being grown near the market?

Correct Answer
✓ A

This applies bid-rent at a global scale. Kenyan highland land is cheap relative to European land. Air freight flattens the distance-cost curve for high-value perishables (a $20 bouquet can absorb the air freight cost that a $0.20/kg grain cannot). The comparative advantage: Kenyan climate produces better flowers more cheaply than Dutch greenhouses, even accounting for transport. This is the globalization of agriculture's fundamental logic: technology (air freight) allows comparative advantage to operate across continental distances, disrupting Von Thünen's original proximity-to-market assumption.

Q16
Unit 5Big Idea: Impact

The "second agricultural revolution" (18th–19th century Britain) and the "Green Revolution" (1960s–70s Asia/Latin America) both dramatically increased agricultural productivity. Which of the following best distinguishes their geographic impacts?

Correct Answer
✓ D

The second agricultural revolution (crop rotation, selective breeding, mechanization) freed up European farm labor that fueled the Industrial Revolution's urban workforce — a direct causal link between agricultural and industrial change. The Green Revolution's impact was different in kind: it raised yields but didn't free labor in the same structural way, because mechanization was limited and the capital divide created inequality rather than structural economic transformation. Understanding these differences is an Impact big idea at its deepest: same category of "revolution," very different geographic and social consequences.

Terrace farming is an ancient intensive agriculture adaptation to land scarcity in mountainous terrain. Q29
Unit 1

Environmental determinism and possibilism represent contrasting views on the relationship between the physical environment and human behavior. Which of the following correctly applies both concepts to the same geographic scenario?

Correct Answer
✓ E

Environmental determinism (Ratzel, Huntington) — physical environment determines cultural outcomes, no choices involved. Possibilism (Vidal de la Blache) — environment sets a range of possibilities; humans choose among them using culture and technology. E correctly applies both to the same case: the Nile. Determinism says flood control → centralization was inevitable; possibilism says flood control was a challenge to which Egyptians chose a particular cultural response. The same environment could have produced different cultural outcomes (as it did in different Nile-valley periods).

Food sovereignty — the principle that communities and nations have the right to define their own food systems, agricultural policies, and what they grow and eat — directly challenges the WTO's free trade model, which treats food as a commodity that should flow to wherever it can be produced most cheaply through comparative advantage. Food sovereignty advocates (Via Campesina movement) argue that allowing global commodity markets to determine what farmers grow and what people eat destroys local agricultural diversity, creates dangerous dependencies on import supply chains, and prioritizes corporate profit over community nutritional security. For developing nations, food sovereignty would mean protecting domestic markets from subsidized foreign imports — precisely what WTO rules restrict.

GExplainhow the data in Stimulus 2 illustrates the difference between the rank-size rule and the primate city model as geographic theories of urban hierarchy.
Scoring Guide — Part G (1 point)

To earn the point: Define or distinguish both models AND apply both to the data.

The rank-size rule predicts that a country's nth-largest city will be 1/n the size of the largest city — producing a balanced urban hierarchy where cities of many sizes coexist in a logarithmic distribution. The United States and Germany approximate this: New York (1.4× Los Angeles) and Berlin (2× Hamburg) reflect a graduated hierarchy of comparable-sized cities. The primate city model predicts one city dominates overwhelmingly, concentrating economic and political activity. Thailand (14:1) and Argentina (~10:1) conform to the primate pattern, where Bangkok and Buenos Aires each represent a disproportionate share of national urban population. The same data thus confirms that different national economic and historical contexts produce fundamentally different urban hierarchy structures — the two models are not competing theories but descriptions of two genuinely different types of urban systems.
